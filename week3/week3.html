Chunking

I chose re/code as an example of chunking because of how well their site's features hide themselves and expand as necessary. This screenshot shows how the two largest social media sites, Facebook and Twitter, are given menu icons. When the user mouses over the down triangle affordance, they get a complete menu on how to follow the site and subscribe to their email list.

Signal-to-noise ratio

Michigan Radio's site is an excellent example of how to show content in a meaningful and concise way. There is nothing here that I consider clutter, everything is tucked neatly away in intuitive affordances — the user can instantly see the headlines. The content also fits the "F" pattern.

Ockham's razor

Always & Forever Computer Entertainment is a studio based in NYC that came under my rader when I watched their interactive music video for dennis.video.

Rather than bragging about their achievements, they demonstrate their skill by making the logo interactive. When the user clicks on it, it chirps and spins and darts away from the mouse. After a few clicks, it even changes the style of the entire page. Fantastic.

Modularity

What I like about ello.co is its two main streams, "Friends" and "Noise". It places these options on the top right of the page, at the end of the menu. "Friends" is something like a Facebook, and "Noise" is something like a Twitter. Each have a very different feel, and it feels like two distinct sites. I think this is an excellent example of how to use Modularity in designing a social media site. I'm looking forward to the debut of their mobile app as well.

Rule of thirds

The Space Weather Prediction Center at NOAA uses the rule of thirds in displaying several of its products. Diverse customers use this site, from communications and power infrastructor officials to radio operator hobbyists, from citizen scientists to Northern Lights afficionados. The site gives users all of the technical information they need—which can a bit intimidating—in an aesthetically pleasing and concise way.
